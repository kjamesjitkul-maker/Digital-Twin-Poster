Concept

What Is a Digital Twin?

A Digital Twin is a virtual replica of a physical system that mirrors real world operations, enabling simulation, monitoring, and what if analysis without disrupting actual processes.

🪞

Virtual Mirror

A DT creates a synchronized digital counterpart of physical assets such as fleets, warehouses, or entire supply chains. It reflects the current state and can project future states under different conditions.

🔄

Data Driven Feedback Loop

True Digital Twins maintain bidirectional data flow between the physical and virtual worlds. IoT sensors, ERP systems, and TMS platforms feed live data into the model, which can then feed optimized decisions back.

🧪

What If Simulation

The core value proposition: run hypothetical scenarios ("What if fuel costs rise 20%?", "What if we share 3 of our 8 routes?") and see projected outcomes before making real commitments.

📊

Maturity Spectrum

Most logistics DTs today are actually Digital Models (no automated data flow) or Digital Shadows (one way data flow). Only about 16% of supply chain DT implementations achieve full closed loop capabilities (Benhamou et al., 2025).

Data Requirements

What Data Makes a Digital Twin Work?

A DT for Physical Internet onboarding needs data across four layers. Each layer demands specific inputs to produce meaningful, personalized simulation results for a prospective road freight carrier.

1

Network Layer — The Existing PI World

The simulation needs a model of the PI network a company would join. This includes π hub locations and their throughput capacities, transport link topologies between hubs, current traffic flows and demand patterns from active participants, service level agreements and pricing structures, and operational rules governing how freight moves through the network. Sources like the ICONET Zenodo dataset and Speth et al.'s European road freight flow data (covering 1,675 NUTS 3 regions) provide realistic open data for this layer.

2

Entrant Layer — The Prospective Carrier's Profile

Every carrier is different, so the DT must ingest company specific data: fleet size and vehicle types with capacities, current route networks and service areas, customer contracts and delivery time windows, cost structures (fuel, tolls, driver wages, maintenance per km), current load factors and empty run percentages, and operational constraints. European cost benchmarks range from €0.50 to €2.00/km depending on corridor and market conditions (IRU/Upply data). The EU average empty run rate sits around 25% (Eurostat).

3

Integration Simulation Layer — The What If Engine

This layer combines Network and Entrant data to run scenarios. It requires algorithmic inputs: route optimization parameters, load consolidation logic, cost and emission calculation models, and partial participation rulesets (e.g., sharing only selected routes). Routing infrastructure from OpenStreetMap and OpenRouteService, which supports truck specific routing profiles with vehicle dimension constraints, enables realistic distance and travel time calculations.

4

Visualization Layer — The Decision Dashboard

Raw simulation outputs must be translated into comprehensible formats for decision makers. This layer needs UI/UX data: side by side comparison templates (current vs. PI integrated operations), scenario slider parameters, risk indicator thresholds, and summary recommendation logic. The goal is transparency and credibility, because even the best simulation fails if the output cannot be understood or trusted.

Value Proposition

Benefits of a Digital Twin Approach

Evidence from EU pilot programs, academic research, and industry analyses points to substantial gains when DT simulation is applied to logistics network optimization and collaborative transport.

💰

Cost Reduction

The SENSE project simulated PI integration for Carrefour, Casino, and their top 100 French suppliers and demonstrated a 32% economic benefit. McKinsey (2024) reports up to 20% improvement in delivery fulfillment and 10% labor cost reduction from supply chain DTs. Kim et al. (2025) achieved 8.97% inventory cost reduction in a real case study.

🌿

Emission Reduction

The same SENSE simulation projected a 60% GHG reduction and a 50% shift from road to rail. By consolidating loads and eliminating empty runs (currently ~25% of trips in Europe), DT enabled PI networks could significantly cut the carbon footprint of road freight transport.

🔍

Visibility Before Commitment

The central innovation of the DT as onboarding tool concept: carriers can see personalized projections of how their specific operations would change inside a PI network. This addresses the "cold start problem" identified by Andrew Chen (2021), where platforms have no value without users but users will not join without existing value.

🤝

Trust Building Through Trialability

Rogers' Diffusion of Innovation theory identifies trialability as a key adoption factor. A DT simulation provides a "try before you commit" mechanism that lets carriers experiment with PI membership on a limited, risk free basis, directly addressing the trust deficit documented across 10+ research sources.

📦

Higher Load Utilization

DT simulations can project load consolidation scenarios where partially filled trucks from different carriers combine freight. In the proposed framework, a carrier might see their load factor increase from 62% to 87% by participating in network level freight pooling.

🛡️

Risk Free Exploration

Unlike full PI pilot participation, DT simulation requires no operational changes, no data sharing with competitors, and no financial commitment. Carriers retain full control while exploring the potential impact of network membership on their business.

Challenges

The Biggest Barriers

Research across 84+ studies (Karam et al., 2021) and extensive literature reviews reveals deeply interconnected technical, organizational, and structural barriers. These are ranked by citation frequency across the literature corpus.

01

Trust Deficits and Fear of Data Exploitation

Cited in 10+ sources. Companies must share precisely the data they most want to protect: routes, costs, volumes, and customer information. A 2025 study of Slovenian logistics firms described the dynamic as a "prisoner's dilemma" where carriers are willing to share knowledge but not hard resources like vehicles or data. Cruijssen et al. (2007) found that impediments expected by non cooperating firms are actually experienced by cooperating ones.

02

Unfair Cost and Benefit Allocation

Cited in 8+ sources. Finding a reliable party to lead cooperation and constructing a fair allocation mechanism for benefits were the top rated impediments in Cruijssen et al.'s seminal survey. Van Duin et al. (2023) found fair cost benefit allocation was significant across all four stakeholder perspectives identified through Q methodology.

03

Loss of Control and Autonomy

Cited in 7+ sources. Pan et al. (2019) found that big trucking companies specifically prefer solutions maintaining their independence and autonomy. Basso et al. (2019) identified loss of control over resources and decision making as a core behavioral barrier.

04

Missing Business Models

Cited in 6+ sources. Sternberg and Norrman (2017) argue the barriers are fundamentally structural rather than merely informational. They found "a lack of understanding of the business models needed that can involve critical actors and promote the adoption of the PI concept."

05

Data Interoperability Crisis

Supply chains generate data from ERP, TMS, WMS, and IoT systems operating on incompatible standards. Elhajj (2026) found protocol mismatches cause a 30% increase in integration time. Pan et al. (2021) reported 71% of logistics organizations have difficulties exchanging data between key platforms.

06

SME Cost Barriers

95.5% of US carriers operate 10 or fewer trucks. Small scale DT implementations start at $50,000 to $100,000, while enterprise deployments reach $500,000 to $2M+. SMEs face a triple disadvantage: insufficient capital, lack of technical talent, and low digital maturity. DTaaS models and the projected $399B DTaaS market by 2034 offer a potential path forward.

07

PI's Own Immaturity

The Physical Internet remains primarily conceptual with limited pilots. The ALICE Roadmap targets PI like operations by 2030 and fully autonomous PI networks by 2040. No PI governance body yet exists. Gartner projects DTs remain 5 to 10 years from mainstream logistics adoption.

Looking Forward

The Path Forward

Federated DT architectures, DTaaS delivery models, privacy preserving computation, and the "come for the tool, stay for the network" platform strategy offer a convergent path. A DT framework that first offers carriers standalone value before requiring multi party participation could bridge the gap between PI's promise and real world adoption.
