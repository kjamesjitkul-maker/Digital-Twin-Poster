Can a Digital Twin convince trucking companies to join the Physical Internet?

Investigating whether a simulation framework that lets road freight carriers preview their operations inside a shared logistics network can reduce the trust deficit and operational uncertainty that currently block adoption.

Hypothesis
Research Hypothesis

A Digital Twin onboarding simulation framework that models a prospective road freight carrier's operations within an existing Physical Internet network and presents personalized what-if scenarios (route optimization, load consolidation, cost and emission projections) will significantly increase the carrier's openness to further dialogue and exploration of PI network membership by reducing perceived operational uncertainty and trust barriers.

This research follows a Design Science Research methodology (Peffers et al. 2007) and addresses a gap that, to the best of my knowledge, no existing publication has explored: using a Digital Twin specifically as a pre-adoption onboarding tool for trucking companies considering Physical Internet network membership.

Concept 1

What is a Digital Twin?

A virtual replica of a physical system that runs in parallel with the real thing, enabling simulation, prediction, and what-if analysis without affecting actual operations.

A Digital Twin is more than a dashboard or a static model. At its core, it is a continuously updated virtual representation of a real-world entity, whether that is a single truck, an entire fleet, a warehouse, or a network of logistics hubs. The "twin" ingests data from its physical counterpart (through sensors, GPS trackers, enterprise systems) and mirrors the state of the real system closely enough that running scenarios against the virtual version produces results you can trust.

In logistics, Digital Twins have been used to optimize warehouse layouts, predict equipment failures, test production schedules, and stress-test supply chains against disruption scenarios. Major corporations like Ford and Procter & Gamble have deployed supply chain Digital Twins at scale, reporting improvements such as 20% better delivery fulfilment and 30% inventory reduction (McKinsey 2024; WEF Global Lighthouse Network).

The critical distinction is between three levels of digital replication. A Digital Model is a static representation with no automated data flow. A Digital Shadow has one-way automated data flow from the physical to the digital. A true Digital Twin has bidirectional data flow: the physical system feeds the virtual, and the virtual feeds decisions back to the physical. According to Benhamou et al. (2025), only about 16% of logistics DT implementations achieve this full closed-loop capability. Most remain at the model or shadow stage.

Why This Matters for Adoption

If a trucking company could see a realistic digital replica of their own fleet operating inside a Physical Internet network, complete with projected cost savings, route changes, and load consolidation opportunities, they would no longer be asked to trust a brochure. They would be evaluating evidence.

Concept 2

What is the Physical Internet?

A proposed open, shared logistics network where companies collaboratively use hubs, vehicles, and containers, inspired by how the digital internet routes data packets.

Today, most logistics companies operate independently. Each plans its own routes, fills its own trucks, and manages its own warehouses. The result is staggering inefficiency: trucks frequently travel half-empty, companies duplicate routes that overlap, and warehouse capacity sits underutilized because it serves only one firm. The European average for empty truck running is 21.6% of all vehicle kilometers (Eurostat 2024), and the average weight-based load factor hovers around 50% (European Environment Agency).

The Physical Internet proposes fixing this by applying the logic of the digital internet to physical goods movement. Just as data packets travel through shared routers and servers regardless of who sent them, physical goods would travel through shared hubs (called π-hubs), trucks (π-movers), and standardized containers (π-containers) regardless of which company owns the freight. Companies would share logistics infrastructure, consolidate loads across organizational boundaries, and route shipments through the most efficient path across a collaborative network.

The concept is well-researched and widely considered promising. The EU's ALICE roadmap targets Physical Internet-like operations by 2030 and fully autonomous PI networks by 2040. Major EU projects including ICONET, CLUSTERS 2.0, SENSE, and the ongoing DISCO initiative have progressively built toward this vision.

The problem is that almost nobody is actually joining.

The Core Problem

Why Carriers Won't Join: The Trust and Uncertainty Barrier

The single largest obstacle to Physical Internet adoption is not technology. It is the inability to see what will happen to your business if you join.

The literature documents a paradox at the heart of PI adoption that I call the trust and data sovereignty paradox. A Digital Twin needs sensitive operational data (routes, costs, volumes, capacity utilization, customer information) to demonstrate the value of joining a PI network. But companies refuse to share that data until they already trust the network. The very tool that could build trust requires trust to exist before it can function.

This is not an abstract concern. Cruijssen, Cools and Dullaert (2007) conducted the first large-scale empirical survey of logistics service providers on horizontal cooperation and found that the top-rated impediment was "finding a reliable party to lead the cooperation." Karam, Reinau and Østergaard (2021) catalogued 31 barriers across five domains preventing collaborative transport network adoption, with trust in coordinators and unfair benefit allocation ranking among the highest.

The Barriers, Organized

🔒

Trust Deficit

Companies fear data exploitation, unfair benefit distribution, and loss of competitive advantage. The prisoner's dilemma: everyone benefits from sharing, but nobody wants to share first.

👁️

Operational Uncertainty

Carriers cannot see how their specific routes, costs, or fleet operations would change inside a PI network. They are asked to leap into the unknown based on generic promises.

🏗️

Missing Business Models

Sternberg & Norrman (2017) identified "a lack of understanding of the business models needed that can involve critical actors." Nobody has shown how to make money as a PI participant.

⚖️

Regulatory & Legal

EU Antitrust restrictions on information sharing among competitors, GDPR data governance requirements across organizational boundaries, and cabotage regulations all create friction.

💸

Cost for SMEs

95.5% of US carriers operate 10 or fewer trucks. DT implementations start at $50,000+. These firms lack capital, technical talent, and digital infrastructure.

🔄

Path Dependency

Existing contracts, systems, and relationships create inertia. Many companies see no urgent reason to change when the current system, despite inefficiency, is familiar.

The Central Insight

Most of these barriers trace back to a single root cause: you cannot see what PI membership would look like for your company. A carrier with 20 trucks and 8 regular routes has no way to preview how those specific operations would transform inside a shared network. They are asked to invest time, resources, and data on faith.

The Proposed Solution

How a Digital Twin Can Bridge the Gap

The idea is simple: build a virtual version of a carrier's operations, plug it into a virtual version of a PI network, and show them what happens.

The proposed framework follows a 4-layer architecture designed to turn the abstract promise of the Physical Internet into a concrete, personalized preview for any trucking company considering membership.

1

Network Layer

Represents the existing PI network: its hubs, transport links, capacity, demand patterns, active participants, and operational rules. This is the "world" into which the prospective company would be integrated.

2

Entrant Layer

A digital profile of the specific trucking company considering membership. Their fleet size, vehicle types, current routes, customer contracts, cost structure, fuel consumption, load factors, and empty-run percentages. Every carrier is different; the projections must be personalized.

3

Integration Simulation Layer

The computational engine that runs what-if scenarios combining Layers 1 and 2. It generates projections: route changes, load consolidation opportunities, cost reductions, emission savings, and partial participation scenarios. This is the trust-building mechanism.

4

Visualization & Decision-Support Layer

The dashboard that translates raw simulation outputs into comprehensible visuals: side-by-side comparisons, scenario sliders, risk indicators. Even the best simulation is useless if decision-makers cannot understand the results.

This approach borrows a proven strategy from platform economics: "come for the tool, stay for the network." First, offer individual fleet optimization and cost modeling as standalone value. Then, as carriers see how their operations could integrate with others, the framework naturally demonstrates network effects and builds the trust needed to convert skeptics into participants.

The Data Question

What Data Must a Carrier Share?

An accurate Digital Twin requires roughly 80 to 100 distinct data parameters. Here is what a trucking company would need to provide, and what open sources can supplement.

One of the immediate concerns from any carrier considering this framework is: what data do I have to hand over? The answer is nuanced. The DT needs enough information to model your operations realistically, but it does not require full access to every business secret. The framework is designed with abstraction layers so that carriers can start with high-level data and only share more detail as trust develops.

Fleet and Vehicle Data

Number of vehicles, types (rigid trucks, articulated tractor-trailers, mega-trailers), cargo capacities in tonnes and cubic meters, fuel type and consumption rates, EURO emission standard class. These parameters allow the simulation to model how your specific vehicles would perform on shared routes. European benchmarks: a standard Euro VI 40-tonne tractor-trailer consumes approximately 33 to 36 liters per 100 km. Vehicles five years old or newer account for 60.8% of all vehicle kilometers.

Route and Operational Data

Current origin-destination pairs served, delivery schedules and time windows, typical trip distances, service areas. Most EU road freight falls in the 150 to 999 km band. This data allows the simulation to identify which of your routes overlap with existing network traffic and where consolidation is possible.

Cost Structure

A compiled breakdown typically looks like: driver/labor costs at roughly 30% of total, fuel at 25 to 30%, tolls at 10 to 15%, maintenance at 10 to 15%, insurance at 4 to 10%, and vehicle/equipment plus administrative overhead making up the remainder. Overall per-kilometer cost ranges from approximately €0.50 to €2.00 across European markets. The simulation uses these figures to generate cost projections for PI participation versus standalone operation.

Performance Baselines

Current empty-run percentage (EU average: 21.6%), average load factor by weight (EU average: ~50%), on-time delivery rate. These establish the baseline against which PI integration benefits are measured. A carrier with 35% empty running has far more to gain from backhaul optimization than one already at 15%.

What Open Data Can Fill In

Approximately 70 to 75% of the data parameters for a proof-of-concept can be sourced from public datasets: Eurostat road freight statistics, the Speth et al. synthetic European road freight flow dataset (1.5 million directed origin-destination flows), OpenRouteService for truck-aware routing, the GLEC Framework for emission factors, and ICONET project simulation parameters. Only carrier-specific operational data and PI governance rules remain as gaps requiring direct input.

The Proof of Concept

Introducing the Simulation

A browser-based discrete-event simulation of five trucking carriers cooperating across a shared region. It is not a Digital Twin, but it demonstrates why one would work.

Important Distinction

This simulation is not a Digital Twin. A true Digital Twin requires real-time bidirectional data from a physical system. This simulation uses synthetic data and simplified physics (straight-line distances instead of road networks, abstract cargo units instead of weighted pallets). What it is is a proof of concept that demonstrates the core mechanism: when you can see, in quantified and visual terms, how cooperation changes your fleet's performance, the abstract concept of "joining a shared network" becomes a concrete, evaluable proposition.

The simulation models five carriers (A through E), each with a home depot and a set of randomly assigned customer delivery orders scattered across a Benelux-inspired map. It compresses a full working day into 5 minutes of real time. The user runs the same orders under different cooperation modes and directly compares the outcomes: how does completion time change? How does load utilization shift? How many empty return trips get eliminated?

It serves as Layer 3 (Integration Simulation) and Layer 4 (Visualization and Decision-Support) of the proposed 4-layer DT framework. The simulation was designed to be shown to industry experts during scenario-based interviews, where they evaluate whether seeing personalized PI integration results would shift their willingness to explore network membership.

How the Simulation Works

Core Mechanics

The simulation runs at 20 ticks per second (a 50ms interval). At each tick, every truck updates its position based on its current state. Trucks move at 1.3 pixels per tick, which at the simulation's 288x time compression translates to roughly 80 km/h in simulated speed. Each customer delivery takes 250 ticks (12.5 real seconds, representing 1 simulated hour of unloading). Each customer node can accommodate a maximum of 3 trucks unloading simultaneously; additional arrivals queue in first-in-first-out order.

The fundamental constraint is the same across all modes: each truck carries exactly one order at a time, must physically travel to the customer node, unloads for a fixed duration, and the node has a 3-truck docking capacity. The only variable between modes is what trucks do between deliveries.

The Controls

  1. Set generates a new random set of delivery orders based on the order range sliders (configurable from 5 to 300 per carrier). This creates the workload for all subsequent runs until Set is clicked again.
  2. Start begins the simulation clock. Trucks begin picking up and delivering orders from their home depots.
  3. Stop / Resume pauses and resumes the simulation, preserving all state. Restart resets all truck positions and counters but keeps the same order assignments. This is the critical comparison mechanism.
  4. Mode toggle switches between Normal, PI 1, PI 2, PI 2.1, and PI 3. Modes cannot be changed while running. The comparison workflow is: Set → run Normal → Restart → switch mode → run again → compare in the Result tab.
  5. Speed buttons (1x, 2x, 4x) control playback speed. The simulation logic is identical; only the visual update rate changes. At 4x, a full run completes in about 75 seconds.

Reading the Display

The simulation screen has three regions. On the left, the control panel holds all buttons, order range sliders, and per-carrier fleet size sliders (1 to 50 trucks each). The center contains the SVG map showing depot nodes (colored rounded rectangles labeled RL1 through RL5), customer nodes (numbered circles 1 through 20), moving truck dots, and route lines. On the right, the KPI dashboard shows real-time metrics for each carrier and the network overall.

Route lines: solid lines in a carrier's color indicate own deliveries. Dashed lines indicate cooperation (assisting another carrier or performing a backhaul). Faint dotted lines show empty return trips. Truck dots: always the carrier's own color with a white stroke. A green ring appears when the truck is helping another carrier. Labels beside each truck show what cargo it is carrying and where it is headed. Customer nodes: dark when empty, indigo when occupied, amber border when full (3 out of 3 docks occupied).

The Tabs

Simulation tab is the main view. When PI 2.1 or PI 3 is active, a collapsible lower panel appears with sub-tabs. The Decisions sub-tab shows every truck as an individual circle with ring colors indicating load status (green for loaded, red for empty, amber for pickup, grey for idle), the current task below, and predicted next action above. The Network sub-tab displays a mini-map of active cooperation arcs between depots. The Nodes sub-tab provides a grid of all 20 customer nodes showing dock occupancy, unloading progress, and queue depth. In PI 3 mode, a fourth Dispatch sub-tab shows routing decision statistics.

The Connections tab is a static diagram showing which carriers deliver to which customer nodes, and which nodes are shared between multiple carriers. The Comms tab is a full-page scrollable log of every routing decision (chain backhauls, smart backhauls, assists, reroutes, interceptions). The Result tab stores completed simulation results with full per-carrier breakdowns, selection checkboxes, delete and share functions.

Key Metrics Explained

Load Utilization is the primary efficiency metric: the percentage of total distance traveled with cargo onboard. A truck driving 100 pixels to a customer (loaded) and 100 pixels home (empty) has 50% utilization. Normal mode is structurally limited to roughly 50%; PI cooperation modes break this ceiling by filling empty return legs.

Points measure contribution. Own deliveries earn 100 points. Assisted or backhaul deliveries split 90 points to the carrier whose truck did the work and 10 to the carrier whose order was fulfilled. Completion time records when each carrier's full order book was delivered. The simulation runs a maximum of 5 real minutes; if orders remain when the timer expires, the result is marked as timed out.

Carrier Focus Filter: each carrier card in the KPI panel has an eye icon. Click it to highlight that carrier's trucks and routes on the map while dimming everything else. Multiple carriers can be focused simultaneously.

Cooperation Levels

The PI Modes Explained

Five modes, from zero cooperation to full dispatch coordination. Each one adds a specific capability and demonstrates a measurable improvement.

Normal

No Cooperation

Each carrier operates completely independently. A truck picks up its next order from the depot, drives to the customer node, unloads, then drives back to the depot empty. If no orders remain, the truck idles. Trucks never interact with other carriers' orders, depots, or trucks.

This represents traditional logistics. Every delivery trip produces one empty return trip, capping the theoretical maximum utilization at 50%. If Carrier A finishes early, its trucks sit idle while Carrier D might still be overwhelmed.

PI 1

Idle Assist

Everything from Normal, plus: when a carrier finishes all its own orders, its idle trucks travel to other carriers' depots, pick up their pending orders, and deliver on their behalf. The helper physically travels to the foreign depot (empty), spends 4 real seconds loading freight, then delivers to the customer.

This is the simplest PI cooperation: carriers with spare capacity lend trucks to overloaded carriers. Total completion time drops because workload is shared. However, utilization stays similar to Normal because assist trips still produce empty legs (travel to the foreign depot, then loaded delivery, then empty return).

PI 2

Fixed-Radius Backhaul

Everything from PI 1, plus: after completing its own delivery, before returning home empty, a truck checks if any nearby carrier depot has a pending order destined for one of the 4 customer nodes closest to the truck's home depot. If found, the truck detours to pick up the cargo and delivers it on the way home, filling what would otherwise be an empty return leg.

This is backhaul optimization, the most commercially proven form of logistics cooperation. The limitation is the fixed-radius filter: only the 4 nearest nodes to each depot qualify as backhaul destinations (20% of all possible nodes). The filter exists to prevent trucks from taking detours longer than the direct trip home.

PI 2.1

Smart Directional Backhaul + Chaining

Three improvements over PI 2:

Smart directional backhaul replaces the fixed 4-node filter. The algorithm evaluates every available order across all foreign carriers and computes a detour ratio: (distance to foreign depot + depot to customer + customer to home) ÷ (direct distance home). If the ratio is below 1.8, the detour is worthwhile. It picks the option with the lowest ratio. This opens up dramatically more backhaul opportunities because any order "on the way home" qualifies.

Chain backhauls (up to 3 consecutive): after completing a backhaul delivery, instead of going home, the truck checks for another opportunity from its current position. A single trip can look like: own delivery → backhaul 1 → backhaul 2 → backhaul 3 → return home. Each link fills what would have been an empty leg.

Universal backhaul trigger: PI 2 only checks for backhaul after own deliveries. PI 2.1 also checks after assist deliveries, meaning trucks helping other carriers look for backhaul opportunities on the way home.

PI 3

Full Dispatch Coordination

PI 3 adds four major optimizations on top of PI 2.1, representing a centrally coordinated dispatch system where carriers share real-time information about node congestion, truck positions, and order availability:

Nearest-first dispatch with anti-clustering. Instead of serving orders in random array sequence, each truck scores all remaining orders by distance plus a congestion penalty (200px per queued truck at the target node) plus a clustering penalty (150px per truck already assigned to that node this tick). The lowest score wins. This prevents 25 trucks from simultaneously targeting the same nearby node at simulation start.

Smartest-assist selection. When a truck looks for a carrier to assist, it calculates the total route cost (distance to foreign depot + depot to best available order) for every carrier with pending orders and picks the combination with the lowest total, instead of always helping Carrier A first.

Unlimited chain backhauls. The hard cap of 3 is removed. The detour ratio (1.8) is the only limiter. A truck can chain 5, 6, or more backhauls as long as each new detour stays efficient. The ratio naturally kills chains when the truck drifts too far from home.

En-route interception. A truck driving home empty checks every ~1 second: is there a nearby depot with a pending order where the full trip costs less than 1.6x the direct home distance? If yes, the truck diverts immediately, converting an empty return into a productive delivery without going all the way home first.

Onboarding Feature

OBS Mode: See What Joining Means for Your Company

The OBS (Onboarding Simulation) mode is the feature designed specifically for the research interviews. It answers the question every prospective carrier asks: "What would change for me?"

When the OBS button is activated, Carrier E is designated as "YOUR COMPANY" on the map. Its depot shows "YOU" instead of "RL5" and gets a distinctive orange border. A Joined/Separate toggle appears in the header.

In Separate mode, Carrier E operates completely alone while the other four carriers (A through D) cooperate with each other using PI 2.1 or PI 3 logic. This represents the current state: your company is on the outside while a PI network operates without you.

In Joined mode, Carrier E participates fully in the network. Other carriers' trucks can help yours when you are overloaded. Your trucks can perform backhauls using other carriers' orders. The full cooperation suite applies to your company.

The comparison workflow is straightforward. Toggle OBS on, click Set to generate orders, run with Separate, then toggle to Joined (the same orders are preserved automatically), run again, and compare the two results in the Result tab. The differences in Carrier E's utilization, completion time, assists received, and backhauls performed are entirely attributable to network membership.

OBS also includes a base mode selector (PI 2.1 or PI 3), allowing users to compare which cooperation architecture benefits the prospect more. This mirrors the real decision a carrier would face: what level of data sharing and coordination commitment is right for us?
